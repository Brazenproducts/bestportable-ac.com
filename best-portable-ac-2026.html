Frequently Asked Questions

How many BTUs do I need for my room?

As a general rule, you need about 20 BTUs per square foot. A 300 sq ft room needs about 6,000-8,000 BTU, while a 500 sq ft room needs 10,000-14,000 BTU. Factor in ceiling height, sun exposure, and how many heat-generating devices are in the room.

Are portable ACs as good as window units?

Window units are generally more efficient for the same BTU rating because they vent heat directly outside. Portable ACs lose some efficiency through the exhaust hose. However, portable ACs offer flexibility — you can move them between rooms and don't need permanent installation. For renters or people who need cooling in multiple rooms, portable wins on convenience.

Single hose vs dual hose — which is better?

Dual hose units are more efficient because they don't create negative pressure in the room. Single hose units pull room air to cool the condenser and exhaust it outside, which draws warm air in through gaps. Dual hose units use outside air for the condenser. That said, quality single-hose units still cool effectively — the difference matters most in very hot climates. Read our full comparison →

How loud are portable air conditioners?

Most portable ACs run between 50-56 dB, which is similar to a normal conversation. Inverter models like the LG Dual Inverter can be as quiet as 44 dB on low settings. For reference, 40 dB is a quiet library and 60 dB is a normal conversation. If noise matters, look for units with a dedicated sleep/night mode.

Do portable ACs need to be vented?

Yes, all portable ACs need to vent hot air outside through a window, sliding door, or wall opening. The exhaust hose removes the heat extracted from your room. "Ventless" evaporative coolers (swamp coolers) exist but are not true air conditioners — they add humidity and only work in dry climates.

How much electricity does a portable AC use?

A typical 8,000 BTU portable AC uses about 900-1,100 watts, costing roughly $0.10-$0.15 per hour at average electricity rates. A 14,000 BTU unit uses 1,300-1,500 watts. Inverter models use 20-30% less electricity than traditional compressor units over time.
